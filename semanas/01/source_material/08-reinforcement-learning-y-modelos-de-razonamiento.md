# Reinforcement Learning y modelos de razonamiento

We have now covered two major stages of training of large language models. In the first stage called the pre-training stage we are basically training on internet documents and you get what's called a base model, basically an internet document simulator. We saw that in the second stage we can actually construct an assistant in the process of post training, specifically in the process of supervised fine-tuning. What I'd like to do now is cover the last and major stage of this pipeline: reinforcement learning.

## Analogia con la escuela

I would now like to try to motivate the reinforcement learning stage with something that you're probably familiar with: going to school. Just like you went to school to become really good at something, we want to take large language models through school.

When we're working with textbooks in school you'll see that there are three major classes of information. The first thing you'll see is a lot of exposition. It's kind of like background knowledge, etc. As you are reading through the words of this exposition you can think of that roughly as training on that data. That's why when you're reading through this stuff, this background knowledge, it's kind of equivalent to pre-training. It's where we build a knowledge base.

The next major kind of information that you will see is problems with their worked solutions. A human expert has given us not just a problem but has also worked through the solution. The solution is basically equivalent to having the ideal response for an assistant. As we are reading the solution we are basically training on the expert data. That roughly corresponds to having the SFT model.

The third stage of reinforcement learning is basically the practice problems. Practice problems of course we know are critical for learning because they're getting you to practice yourself and discover ways of solving these problems yourself. What you get in a practice problem is you get a problem description but you're not given the solution. But you are given the final answer usually in the answer key of the textbook. So you know the final answer that you're trying to get to. You have the problem statement but you don't have the solution. You are trying to practice the solution. You're trying out many different things and you're seeing what gets you to the final solution the best. You're discovering how to solve these problems. That's what reinforcement learning is about.

## Por que no basta con SFT

Going back to the problem that we worked with previously: "Emily buys three apples and two oranges. Each orange is $2. The total cost of all the fruit is $13. What is the cost of each apple?" Here are four possible candidate solutions as an example. They all reach the answer three.

What I'd like you to appreciate at this point is that if I am the human data labeler that is creating a conversation to be entered into the training set, I don't actually really know which of these conversations to add to the data set. Some of these conversations kind of set up a system of equations, some of them sort of talk through it in English, and some of them just kind of skip right through to the solution.

The first purpose of a solution is to reach the right answer of course. But there's a secondary purpose where we are also just kind of trying to make it nice for the human. We need to differentiate between these two. If we only care about the final answer then which of these is the optimal or the best solution for the LLM to reach the right answer? We don't know.

What's easy or hard for you and I as human labelers is different than what's easy or hard for the LLM. Its cognition is different. Some of the token sequences that are trivial for me might be too much of a leap for the LLM. Conversely many of the tokens that I'm creating might be just trivial to the LLM and we're just wasting tokens. So we are not in a good position to create these token sequences for the LLM. They're useful by imitation to initialize the system, but we really want the LLM to discover the token sequences that work for it. It needs to find for itself what token sequence reliably gets to the answer given the prompt. It needs to discover that in the process of reinforcement learning and of trial and error.

## Como funciona RL

The way that reinforcement learning will basically work is actually quite simple. We need to try many different kinds of solutions and we want to see which solutions work well or not. We're basically going to take the prompt, we're going to run the model, and the model generates a solution. Then we're going to inspect the solution and we know that the correct answer for this one is $3.

We delete this and rerun it again. The model solves it in a slightly different way. Every single attempt will be a different generation because these models are stochastic systems. Remember that at every single token here we have a probability distribution and we're sampling from that distribution so we end up going down slightly different paths.

In practice you might actually sample thousands of independent solutions or even like a million solutions for just a single prompt. Some of them will be correct and some of them will not be very correct. Basically what we want to do is we want to encourage the solutions that lead to correct answers. Whatever token sequences happened in the red solutions, obviously something went wrong along the way somewhere and this was not a good path to take through the solution. Whatever token sequences there were in the green solutions, well things went pretty well in this situation and we want to do more things like it in prompts like this.

The way we encourage this kind of behavior in the future is we basically train on these sequences. But these training sequences now are not coming from expert human annotators. There's no human who decided that this is the correct solution. This solution came from the model itself. The model is practicing here. It tried out a few solutions, four of them seem to have worked, and now the model will kind of train on them. This corresponds to a student basically looking at their solutions and being like "okay well this one worked really well, so this is how I should be solving these kinds of problems."

We're going to run many different diverse prompts across lots of math problems and physics problems and whatever: tens of thousands of prompts, thousands of solutions per prompt. And as we're iterating this process the model is discovering for itself what kinds of token sequences lead it to correct answers. It's not coming from a human annotator. The model is kind of playing in this playground and it knows what it's trying to get to and it's discovering sequences that work for it. These are sequences that don't make any mental leaps, they seem to work reliably and statistically, and fully utilize the knowledge of the model as it has it. This is the process of reinforcement learning.

## DeepSeek R1 y la emergencia del pensamiento

The first two stages (pre-training and supervised fine-tuning) have been around for years and they are very standard. It is this last stage, the RL training, that is a lot more early in its process of development and is not standard yet in the field.

That's why the paper from DeepSeek that came out very recently was such a big deal. This is a paper from this company called DeepSeek AI in China. This paper really talked very publicly about reinforcement learning fine-tuning for large language models and how incredibly important it is and how it brings out a lot of reasoning capabilities in the models. This paper reinvigorated the public interest of using RL for LLMs and gave a lot of the nitty-gritty details that are needed to reproduce their results.

Looking at the improvement in how the models are solving mathematical problems: in the beginning they're not doing very well but then as you update the model with many thousands of steps their accuracy kind of continues to climb. The models are improving and they're solving these problems with a higher accuracy as you do this trial and error on a large data set of these kinds of problems.

Even more incredible than the quantitative results is the qualitative means by which the model achieves these results. Later on in the optimization the model seems to be using more tokens to get its higher accuracy results. It's learning to create very very long solutions. Why are these solutions very long?

Basically what they discover is that the model learns to do stuff like this: "wait wait wait, that's not right. Let me re-evaluate this step by step to identify the correct sum." What is the model doing here? The model is basically re-evaluating steps. It has learned that it works better for accuracy to try out lots of ideas, try something from different perspectives, retrace, reframe, backtrack. It's doing a lot of the things that you and I are doing in the process of problem solving for mathematical questions. But it's rediscovering what happens in your head, not what you put down on the solution.

There is no human who can hardcode this stuff in the ideal assistant response. This is only something that can be discovered in the process of reinforcement learning because you wouldn't know what to put here. This just turns out to work for the model and it improves its accuracy in problem solving. So the model learns what we call these chains of thought in your head and it's an emergent property of the optimization. That's what's bloating up the response length, but that's also what's increasing the accuracy.

What's incredible here is basically the model is discovering ways to think. It's learning what I like to call cognitive strategies of how you manipulate a problem, how you approach it from different perspectives, how you pull in some analogies, how you try out many different things over time, check a result from different perspectives, and how you kind of solve problems. Here it's kind of discovered by the RL. Extremely incredible to see this emerge in the optimization without having to hardcode it anywhere. The only thing we've given it are the correct answers and this comes out from trying to just solve them correctly.

## Modelos de razonamiento en la practica

When I pasted the problem into ChatGPT 4o I get a standard response. Let's take a look at what happens when you give this same query to what's called a reasoning or a thinking model -- this is a model that was trained with reinforcement learning. The DeepSeek R1 model is available on chat.deepseek.com. With the "deep think" button turned on you get the R1 model.

Here's what we get from the RL model: "Let me try to figure this out. Emily buys three apples and two oranges, each orange costs $2, total is 13. I need to find out..." As you're reading this you can't escape thinking that this model is thinking. It definitely is pursuing the solution. It derives that it must cost $3, and then it says "wait a second, let me check my math again to be sure" and then it tries it from a slightly different perspective and then it says "yep all that checks out. I think that's the answer. I don't see any mistakes. Let me see if there's another way to approach the problem, maybe setting up an equation..." Then once it did the thinking process it writes up the nice solution for the human. So this thinking process is more about the correctness aspect and the final write-up is more about the presentation aspect.

The models that you're going to see in the ChatGPT dropdown -- some of them like o1, o3-mini, o3-mini-high, etc. -- they are talking about "uses advanced reasoning." What this is referring to is the fact that it was trained by reinforcement learning with techniques very similar to those of DeepSeek R1. These are thinking models trained with RL. Models like GPT-4 or GPT-4o mini that you're getting in the free tier, you should think of them as mostly SFT models (supervised fine tuning models). They don't actually do this "thinking" as you see in the RL models.

## AlphaGo y el poder del RL

The discovery that reinforcement learning is an extremely powerful way of learning is not new to the field of AI. One place we've already seen this demonstrated is in the game of Go and famously DeepMind developed the system AlphaGo. In this paper, they compared the strength of a model trained by supervised learning and a model trained by reinforcement learning. The supervised learning model is imitating human expert players. If you just get a huge amount of games played by expert players in the game of Go and you try to imitate them, you are going to get better but then you top out and you never quite get better than some of the top top top players like Lee Sedol.

But the process of reinforcement learning is significantly more powerful. In reinforcement learning for a game of Go it means that the system is playing moves that empirically and statistically lead to winning the game. AlphaGo is a system where it kind of plays against itself and it's using reinforcement learning. The system is learning basically the sequences of actions that empirically and statistically lead to winning the game. Reinforcement learning is not going to be constrained by human performance and can do significantly better and overcome even the top players.

The famous "Move 37" in AlphaGo is referring to a specific point in time where AlphaGo basically played a move that no human expert would play. The probability of this move to be played by a human player was evaluated to be about 1 in 10,000. But in retrospect it was a brilliant move. AlphaGo in the process of reinforcement learning discovered a strategy of playing that was unknown to humans. In principle we can actually see the equivalence of that if we continue scaling this paradigm in language models.

## RLHF: RL en dominios no verificables

So far all of the problems that we've looked at are in what's called verifiable domains. Any candidate solution we can score very easily against a concrete answer. The problem is that we can't apply the strategy in what's called unverifiable domains. Usually these are for example creative writing tasks like "write a joke about pelicans" or "write a poem" or "summarize a paragraph." In these kinds of domains it becomes harder to score our different solutions.

For example writing a joke about pelicans: we can generate lots of different jokes of course. The problem that we are facing is how do we score them. In principle we could get a human to look at all these jokes. The problem is that if you are doing reinforcement learning you're going to be doing many thousands of updates and for each update you want to be looking at thousands of prompts and for each prompt you want to be looking at hundreds or thousands of different generations. So there's just like way too many of these to look at.

One solution was proposed in a paper that introduced what's called Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). The core trick is that of indirection. We're going to involve humans just a little bit. The way we cheat is that we basically train a whole separate neural network that we call a reward model and this neural network will kind of imitate human scores. We're going to ask humans to score rollouts, then imitate human scores using a neural network. This neural network will become a kind of simulator of human preferences. Now that we have a neural network simulator we can do RL against it. Instead of asking a real human we're asking a simulated human for their score of a joke as an example. Once we have a simulator we can query it as many times as we want and it's a whole automatic process.

We're going to ask a human to order these jokes from the best to worst. We're asking humans to order instead of give scores directly because it's a bit of an easier task. The reward model will take as input the prompt and a candidate joke. The output of a reward model is a single number (a score that can range from zero to one). We compare the scores given by the reward model with the ordering given by the human and update the model. After many updates, the reward model becomes a better and better simulator of human preferences, which we can then do RL against.

## Limitaciones de RLHF

RLHF also comes with significant downsides. The main one is that basically we are doing reinforcement learning not with respect to humans and actual human judgment, but with respect to a lossy simulation of humans. And something even more subtle and devious: reinforcement learning is extremely good at discovering a way to game the simulation. The reward model is a massive neural net with billions of parameters. It turns out that there are ways to game these models. You can find kinds of inputs that were not part of their training set and these inputs inexplicably get very high scores but in a fake way.

Very often what you find if you run RLHF for very long: in the first few hundred steps the jokes about pelicans are probably improving a little bit and then they actually dramatically fall off the cliff and you start to get extremely nonsensical results. Like for example the top joke about pelicans starts to be "the the the the the the" -- and this makes no sense. But when you take it and you plug it into your reward model, the reward model loves this as a joke. It will give it a score of 1.0. These are what's called adversarial examples.

Fundamentally this is because our scoring function is a giant neural net and RL is extremely good at finding just the ways to trick it. So you always run RLHF for maybe a few hundred updates, the model is getting better, and then you have to crop it and you are done. You can't run too much against this reward model because the optimization will start to game it.

RLHF is not RL in the magical sense. This is not RL that you can run indefinitely. In verifiable domains you can run RL indefinitely. You could run for tens of thousands, hundreds of thousands of steps and discover all kinds of really crazy strategies. In the game of Go there's no way to game the winning of a game. You can do RL indefinitely and you can eventually beat even Lee Sedol. But with reward models which are gameable you cannot repeat this process indefinitely.

I kind of see RLHF as not real RL because the reward function is gameable. It's kind of more in the realm of little fine-tuning. It's a little improvement but it's not something that is fundamentally set up correctly where you can insert more compute, run for longer and get much better and magical results.
