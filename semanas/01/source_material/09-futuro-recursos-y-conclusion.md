# Futuro, recursos y conclusion

## Resumen del pipeline de entrenamiento

So that's most of the technical content that I wanted to cover. I took you through the three major stages and paradigms of training these models: pre-training, supervised fine-tuning, and reinforcement learning. I showed you that they loosely correspond to the process we already use for teaching children. In particular we talked about pre-training being sort of the basic knowledge acquisition of reading exposition, supervised fine-tuning being the process of looking at lots and lots of worked examples and imitating experts, and practice problems.

The only difference is that we now have to effectively write textbooks for LLMs and AIs across all the disciplines of human knowledge and also in all the cases where we actually would like them to work, like code and math and all the other disciplines. We're in the process of writing textbooks for them, refining all the algorithms, and doing a really good job at the execution of training these models at scale and efficiently.

I also talked about the theory of mind of these models. The thing I want you to take away is that these models are really good but they're extremely useful as tools for your work. You shouldn't trust them fully. Even though we have mitigations for hallucinations, the models are not perfect and they will hallucinate still. It's gotten better over time and it will continue to get better, but they can hallucinate.

In addition to that I covered what I call the Swiss cheese sort of model of LLM capabilities. The models are incredibly good across so many different disciplines but then fail randomly almost in some unique cases. For example "what is bigger 9.11 or 9.9" -- the model doesn't know. But simultaneously it can turn around and solve Olympiad questions. So this is a hole in the Swiss cheese and there are many of them. Don't treat these models as infallible models. Check their work, use them as tools, use them for inspiration, use them for the first draft, but work with them as tools and be ultimately responsible for the product of your work.

## Capacidades futuras

I have a few bullet points on some of the things that you can expect coming down the pipe.

**Multimodalidad.** The models will very rapidly become multimodal. Everything I talked about above concerned text but very soon we'll have LLMs that can not just handle text but they can also operate natively and very easily over audio (so they can hear and speak) and also images (so they can see and paint). We're already seeing the beginnings of all of this but this will be all done natively inside the language model. The reason that this is actually no different from everything we've covered is that as a baseline you can tokenize audio and images and apply the exact same approaches. So it's not a fundamental change. For tokenizing audio we can look at slices of the spectrogram of the audio signal and we can tokenize that and just add more tokens that represent audio. The same for images: we can use patches and separately tokenize patches. An image is just a sequence of tokens.

**Agentes.** Currently most of the work is that we're handing individual tasks to the models on kind of a silver platter. "Please solve this task for me" and the model sort of does this little task. But it's up to us to still organize a coherent execution of tasks to perform jobs. The models are not yet at the capability required to do this in a coherent, error-correcting way over long periods of time. But they're getting there and this is improving over time. What's going to happen is we're going to start to see what's called agents which perform tasks over time. You supervise them and you watch their work and they come up once in a while to report progress. We're going to see more long running agent tasks that don't just take a few seconds of response but many tens of seconds or even minutes or hours over time. These models are not infallible, so all of this will require supervision. In factories people talk about the human-to-robot ratio for automation. I think we're going to see something similar in the digital space where we are going to be talking about human-to-agent ratios where humans become a lot more supervisors of agent tasks in the digital domain.

**Uso de computadoras.** Right now these models aren't able to take actions on your behalf. But if you saw ChatGPT launch the Operator, that's one early example of that where you can actually hand off control to the model to perform keyboard and mouse actions on your behalf.

**Test-time training.** Remember that everything we've done above has two major stages: the training stage where we tune the parameters, and then we fix them and deploy the model for inference. The model is fixed, it doesn't change anymore. The only thing that is changing is the tokens inside the context window. So the only type of learning the model has access to is the in-context learning of its dynamically adjustable context window. But this is still different from humans who actually are able to learn depending on what they're doing. Especially when you sleep for example, your brain is updating your parameters. There's no kind of equivalent of that currently in these models. I think this will be necessary because the context window is a finite and precious resource, especially once we start to tackle very long running multimodal tasks.

## Donde seguir el campo

The three resources that I have consistently used to stay up to date are:

**LM Arena (lmarena.ai).** This is basically an LLM leaderboard and it ranks all the top models. The ranking is based on human comparisons: humans prompt these models and they get to judge which one gives a better answer. They don't know which model is which. You can see the different organizations like Google, OpenAI, etc. For now you can see Google is currently on top with OpenAI right behind, DeepSeek in position number three. The reason this is a big deal is that DeepSeek is an MIT license model, it's open weights, anyone can use these weights. It is kind of unprecedented that a model this strong was released with open weights. I will say that this leaderboard was really good for a long time. I do think that in the last few months it's become a little bit gamed. Use this as a first pass but try out a few of the models for your tasks and see which one performs better.

**AI News newsletter.** AI News is not very creatively named but it is a very good newsletter produced by Swyx and friends. It is extremely comprehensive. It is produced almost every other day. Some of it is written by humans and curated by humans but a lot of it is constructed automatically with LLMs. The summaries all the way up top are quite good and I think have some human oversight.

**X (Twitter).** A lot of AI happens on X. Follow people who you like and trust and get all your latest and greatest on X as well.

## Donde encontrar los modelos

For any of the biggest proprietary models you just have to go to the website of that LLM provider. For OpenAI that's chatgpt.com. For Gemini I think it's gemini.google.com or AI Studio.

For the open weights models like DeepSeek, etc. you have to go to some kind of an inference provider of LLMs. My favorite one is Together (together.ai). When you go to the playground of Together you can sort of pick lots of different models and all of these are open models of different types.

For base models I usually go to Hyperbolic because they serve the Llama 3.1 base model. I love that model and you can just talk to it there. I wish more people hosted base models because they are useful and interesting to work with in some cases.

You can also take some of the models that are smaller and you can run them locally. For example DeepSeek, the biggest model you're not going to be able to run locally on your MacBook, but there are smaller versions of the DeepSeek model that are what's called distilled. You can run these models at smaller precision. My favorite is LM Studio, which is basically an app you can get. Once you actually understand how it works and load up a model, you can just talk to it and all of this happens locally on your computer.

## Conclusion

The question was: when we go to chatgpt.com and we enter some kind of a query and we hit go, what exactly is happening here? What are we seeing, what are we talking to, how does this work? In particular we now know that your query is taken and is first chopped up into tokens. Your query gets inserted into the conversation protocol format, this whole thing ends up being just a token sequence, a one-dimensional token sequence under the hood. ChatGPT saw this token sequence and when we hit go it basically continues appending tokens into this list. It acts like a token autocomplete.

The first stage of this process is the pre-training stage which fundamentally has to do with just knowledge acquisition from the internet into the parameters of this neural network. Where the personality really comes in is in the process of supervised fine-tuning. What happens here is that a company like OpenAI will curate a large data set of conversations. Even though there's a lot of synthetic data generation used throughout this entire process, fundamentally this is a human data curation task with lots of humans involved.

What is the way to think about what came back? I think the right way to think about it is that this is the neural network simulation of a data labeler at OpenAI. It's as if I gave this query to a data labeler at OpenAI who first reads all of the labeling instructions and then spends 2 hours writing up the ideal assistant response to this query. We're not actually doing that; what we're getting here is a neural network simulation of that process. These neural networks don't function like human brains do. They are different. What's easy or hard for them is different from what's easy or hard for humans. We really are just getting a simulation.

Things change a little bit when you actually go and reach for one of the thinking models. GPT-4o basically doesn't do reinforcement learning (it does do RLHF but I've told you that RLHF is not RL -- there's no time for magic in there, it's just a little bit of a fine-tuning). But these thinking models do use RL. They go through this third stage of perfecting their thinking process, discovering new thinking strategies, and solutions to problem solving that look a little bit like your internal monologue.

This reinforcement learning here is still way too new, primordial and nascent. We're just seeing the beginnings of the hints of greatness in the reasoning problems. We're seeing something that is in principle capable of something like the equivalent of Move 37 but not in the game of Go but in open domain thinking and problem solving. In principle these models are capable of analogies no human has had. I think it's incredibly exciting that these models exist but again it's very early.

Overall it is an extremely exciting time to be in the field. I personally use these models all the time daily, tens or hundreds of times, because they dramatically accelerate my work. Be aware of some of their shortcomings. Even with RL models they're going to suffer from some of these issues. Use it as a tool in a toolbox. Don't trust it fully because they will randomly do dumb things. They will randomly hallucinate. They will randomly skip over some mental arithmetic and not get it right. They randomly can't count or something like that. So use them as tools in the toolbox, check their work and own the product of your work. But use them for inspiration, for first draft, ask them questions but always check and verify and you will be very successful in your work if you do so.
