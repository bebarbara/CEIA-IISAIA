# Alucinaciones y uso de herramientas

## Alucinaciones: por que ocurren

Now I want to turn to the topic of LLM psychology as I like to call it, which is what are sort of the emergent cognitive effects of the training pipeline that we have for these models. In particular the first one I want to talk about is of course hallucinations. You might be familiar with model hallucinations: it's when LLMs make stuff up, they just totally fabricate information, etc. It's a big problem with LLM assistants. It is a problem that existed to a large extent with early models from many years ago and I think the problem has gotten a bit better because there are some mitigations that I'm going to go into in a second.

Here's a specific example: consider three conversations that you might have in your training set. These are pretty reasonable conversations that you could imagine being in the training set: "Who is Tom Cruise?" -- well Tom Cruise is a famous American actor and producer, etc. "Who is John Barrasso?" -- this turns out to be a US Senator. "Who is Genghis Khan?" -- well Genghis Khan was blah blah blah. So this is what your conversations could look like at training time.

Now the problem with this is that when the human is writing the correct answer for the assistant in each one of these cases, the human either knows who this person is or they research them on the internet and they come in and they write this response that kind of has this confident tone of an answer. What happens basically is that at test time when you ask for someone who is -- this is a totally random name that I totally came up with and I don't think this person exists -- "Who is Orson Kovats?" The problem is that the assistant will not just tell you "oh I don't know." Even if the assistant and the language model itself might know inside its features, inside its activations, inside of its brain sort of -- it might know that this person is like not someone that it's familiar with. Even if some part of the network kind of knows that in some sense, saying "oh I don't know who this is" is not going to happen. Because the model statistically imitates its training set. In the training set the questions of the form "who is blah" are confidently answered with the correct answer. So it's going to take on the style of the answer and it's going to do its best. It's going to give you statistically the most likely guess and it's just going to basically make stuff up.

## Mitigacion 1: ensenar al modelo a decir "no se"

Let's take a look at for example how Meta dealt with hallucinations for the Llama 3 series of models. In this paper that they published from Meta we can go into hallucinations, which they call here "factuality." They describe the procedure by which they basically interrogate the model to figure out what it knows and doesn't know, to figure out sort of the boundary of its knowledge. Then they add examples to the training set where for the things where the model doesn't know them, the correct answer is that the model doesn't know them.

This sounds like a very easy thing to do in principle but this roughly fixes the issue. The reason it fixes the issue is because the model might actually have a pretty good model of its self-knowledge inside the network. You might imagine that there's a neuron somewhere in the network that sort of lights up for when the model is uncertain. But the problem is that the activation of that neuron is not currently wired up to the model actually saying in words that it doesn't know. So even though the internal of the neural network knows (because there's some neurons that represent that), the model will not surface that. It will instead take its best guess so that it sounds confident, just like it sees in a training set.

The procedure roughly works like this: take a random document in a training set, take a paragraph, use an LLM to construct questions about that paragraph, then interrogate the model. If we interrogate the model three times about a factual question and we compare its answers to the correct answer, and if the model doesn't know, then we know that the model doesn't know this question. Then what we do is we take this question, we create a new conversation in the training set, and when the question is "how many Stanley Cups did he win?" the answer is "I'm sorry I don't know" or "I don't remember." That's the correct answer for this question because we interrogated the model and we saw that that's the case.

If you do this for many different types of questions for many different types of documents, you are giving the model an opportunity to in its training set refuse to say based on its knowledge. If you just have a few examples of that in your training set, the model will know and has the opportunity to learn the association of this knowledge-based refusal to this internal neuron somewhere in its network that we presume exists. It can learn that association that "hey when this neuron of uncertainty is high then I actually don't know, and I'm allowed to say that I'm sorry but I don't think I remember this." If you have these examples in your training set then this is a large mitigation for hallucination. That's roughly speaking why ChatGPT is able to say things like "I don't know."

## Mitigacion 2: uso de herramientas (tool use)

We can actually do much better than that. Instead of just saying that we don't know, we can introduce an additional mitigation to give the LLM an opportunity to be factual and actually answer the question.

What do you and I do if I was to ask you a factual question and you don't know? Well you could go off and do some search and use the internet and you could figure out the answer. We can do the exact same thing with these models.

Think of the knowledge inside the neural network, inside its billions of parameters, think of that as kind of a vague recollection of the things that the model has seen during its training during the pre-training stage a long time ago. Think of that knowledge in the parameters as something you read a month ago. If you keep reading something then you will remember it and the model remembers that. But if it's something rare then you probably don't have a really good recollection of that information. But what you and I do is we just go and look it up. When you go and look it up, what you're doing basically is you're refreshing your working memory with information and then you're able to sort of retrieve it, talk about it, etc. So we need some equivalent of allowing the model to refresh its memory or its recollection and we can do that by introducing tools for the models.

## Busqueda web como herramienta

Instead of just saying "hey I'm sorry I don't know," we can attempt to use tools. We can create a mechanism by which the language model can emit special tokens and these are tokens that we're going to introduce, new tokens. For example here I've introduced two tokens and I've introduced a format or a protocol for how the model is allowed to use these tokens.

For example, instead of answering the question, instead of just saying "I don't know sorry," the model has the option now of emitting the special token "search_start" and this is the query that will go to like Bing.com in the case of OpenAI or Google search. So it will emit the query and then it will emit "search_end." Then here what will happen is that the program that is sampling from the model, that is running the inference, when it sees the special token "search_end," instead of sampling the next token in the sequence it will actually pause generating from the model. It will go off, it will open a session with Bing.com and it will paste the search query into Bing. It will then get all the text that is retrieved and it will basically take that text and it will copy paste it into the context window.

When the text comes there, it enters the context window. So that text from the web search is now inside the context window that will feed into the neural network. You should think of the context window as kind of like the working memory of the model. That data that is in the context window is directly accessible by the model. It directly feeds into the neural network. So it's not anymore a vague recollection; it's data that it has in the context window and is directly available to that model. So now when it's sampling the new tokens afterwards it can reference very easily the data that has been copy pasted in there.

Web search is just one of the tools. Basically you introduce new tokens, you introduce some schema by which the model can utilize these tokens and can call these special functions like web search functions. How do you teach the model how to correctly use these tools like say web search? Well again you do that through training sets. We need now to have a bunch of data and a bunch of conversations that show the model by example how to use web search. If you have a few thousand examples of that in your training set, the model will actually do a pretty good job of understanding how this tool works. Because of the pre-training data set and its understanding of the world, it actually kind of understands what a web search is and it actually kind of has a pretty good native understanding of what kind of stuff is a good search query.

## Conocimiento en parametros vs ventana de contexto

I want to stress one more time this very important sort of psychology point. Knowledge in the parameters of the neural network is a vague recollection. The knowledge in the tokens that make up the context window is the working memory.

Roughly speaking it works kind of like it works for us in our brain. The stuff we remember is our parameters, and the stuff that we just experienced like a few seconds or minutes ago, you can imagine that being in our context window. This context window is being built up as you have a conscious experience around you.

This has a bunch of implications also for your use of LLMs in practice. For example I can go to ChatGPT and I can say "can you summarize chapter one of Jane Austen's Pride and Prejudice?" This is a perfectly fine prompt and ChatGPT actually does something relatively reasonable here. The reason it does that is because ChatGPT has a pretty good recollection of a famous work like Pride and Prejudice. It's probably seen a ton of stuff about it.

But usually when I actually interact with LLMs and I want them to recall specific things, it always works better if you just give it to them. A much better prompt would be something like this: "can you summarize for me chapter one? And then I am attaching it below for your reference" and then I paste it in. I do that because when it's in the context window the model has direct access to it and can exactly -- it doesn't have to recall it, it just has access to it. So this summary can be expected to be a significantly higher quality than the one from memory. I think you and I would work in the same way. You would produce a much better summary if you had reread this chapter before you had to summarize it. That's basically what's happening here.
